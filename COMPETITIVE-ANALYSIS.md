# Competitive & Improvement Analysis — `decodable-readers`

*Analyst review of `PLAN.md` v0.1.0 (2026-06-28). Web research conducted 2026-06-29; sources cited inline.*

---

## 1. Correctness & completeness review of PLAN.md

The plan is unusually rigorous for a v0.1 — it correctly identifies that **provable decodability is the project's identity**, and (like the `vital-info-translations` exemplar) makes it a checkable, fail-closed gate rather than an author claim. That core instinct is right and is the single most defensible design decision in the document. Findings below are ordered roughly by importance.

**(a) Decodability control — the cumulative scope-and-sequence + linter design is sound, but the linter is harder than the plan admits.** The plan's central mechanism — a curated, hand-segmented lexicon (grapheme→phoneme) + a checker that classifies each running word as *decodable / tricky-listed / off-sequence / needs-segmentation*, refusing to guess — is the correct engineering choice and is well-justified (auto-G2P is unreliable for deep orthographies; early vocab is small). Three correctness gaps:
   - **Segmentation is context- and pronunciation-dependent, and the lexicon-as-single-key model under-handles this.** A word's decodability depends on *which* phoneme a grapheme maps to at this step (e.g. `c` = /k/ vs /s/; `g` = /g/ vs /j/; `-ed` = /t/,/d/,/ɪd/; `a` in *was* vs *cat*; *read* present vs past). The schema stores one `segmentation` per `word`, but homographs and heteronyms need *per-sense* entries, and a grapheme→phoneme pair is only "introduced" if *that specific correspondence* is at-or-before step k — not merely the letter. The plan gestures at this ("per the word's segmentation") but the data model (`{word, segmentation, syllables}`) should explicitly key GPCs as (grapheme, phoneme) tuples and allow multiple entries per spelling. **Recommend making this explicit in `lexiconSchema` before M0 ships.**
   - **Tokenization rules are unspecified and load-bearing.** Capitalization, contractions (*can't* → is the apostrophe-t taught?), hyphenates, proper nouns (names are a classic decodable cheat — are character names exempt or must they be decodable?), inflections, and numerals all change coverage. "100% coverage" is only meaningful once these are pinned. **This belongs in the content policy and the checker spec, not left implicit.**
   - **"100% coverage" hard gate may be pedagogically too strict at face value.** Most published decodables (and the research convention) target ~80% decodability with pre-taught tricky words filling the gap (Geodes is "at least 80% decodable" per Great Minds; CKLA markets "100% decodable" *only when used alongside its own sequence*). The plan's 100%-coverage-where-coverage-counts-tricky-words-as-passing is internally consistent (off-sequence = 0; tricky words are allowed and separately capped), but the document conflates "100% decodable" rhetoric with the looser industry meaning in places. The two numbers (coverage vs tricky-density) are the right model — just ensure external messaging doesn't claim something stricter than competitors when the tricky-word cap is doing real work.

**(b) Structured-literacy evidence base — correctly grounded, lightly cited.** The premise (systematic phonics needs decodable connected text; predictable/leveled texts during the phonics phase reward guessing) is well-supported by reading science and is the consensus position (e.g., Reading Universe / Reading Rockets on decodable text). The plan honestly flags citations as "to be cited per adopted sequence." **One nuance to add:** the evidence *base* for decodable texts specifically (vs. systematic phonics generally) is real but more mixed/thinner than the plan implies; positioning the books as "aligned to structured-literacy practice" rather than "proven by RCT" is the safer, more honest claim, and the plan's aspirational/observational treatment of the learner-decoding metric already does this well.

**(c) Story quality vs. constraint tension — acknowledged but under-instrumented.** This is the perennial failure mode of decodables ("the fat cat sat on a mat") and the plan names it via the tricky-density cap. But the cap only prevents *sight-word overload*; it does not measure *narrative quality*. At the earliest levels (s/a/t/p/i/n only — cf. Phonics Hero, Read Write Inc.), a genuinely engaging story is near-impossible, which is why competitors lean on illustration, repetition-with-variation, and humour. **Recommend:** a story-quality rubric item in the review checklist (engagement, character, arc, age-fit) that is *human-judged and gate-blocking*, explicitly separate from decodability. The plan has a safeguarding/representation gate but no *literary-quality* gate; without one, "checker-passed" books can be correct and lifeless.

**(d) Scope-and-sequence choice (no universal standard) — correctly handled.** Research confirms there is **no single "correct" phonics scope-and-sequence** (Literacy Edventures, Keys to Literacy, IMSE all state this; the field agrees only that instruction must be *systematic, sequential, cumulative*). The plan's decision to *support multiple published sequences as data and decline to declare one correct* is exactly right and defuses method-war risk. **Gap:** the plan defers the actual M0 choice to an open question. It should at least name the **shortlist** and selection criteria now, because the lexicon, the first 6 books, and the export pipeline all depend on it. Strong open candidates with transparent, openly-documented orders: **UFLI Foundations** (freely published scope & sequence + free decodable passages, University of Florida) and a **Letters-and-Sounds-derived** order (widely documented, non-proprietary). Avoid aligning to copyrighted commercial orders (Fundations/Wilson, Read Write Inc.) as the *primary* sequence — the plan already flags this correctly.

**(e) Multilingual orthography — the strongest part of the plan.** The "never machine-translate decodables; author natively per language against its own sequence" non-goal is correct and is the project's biggest potential differentiator (see §3–4). The "transparent (shallow) orthographies first" prioritization is well-reasoned: shallow orthographies (e.g., Spanish, Swahili, Finnish, isiXhosa) have near-bijective grapheme→phoneme mappings, making the lexicon/checker dramatically more tractable and segmentation risk far lower. **Minor gaps:** (i) the model is implicitly alphabetic/phonemic — abugidas (Hindi/Amharic), abjads (Arabic with/without diacritics), tonal orthographies, and syllabaries don't fit a flat GPC list cleanly; the schema should either declare alphabetic-only scope for v1 or accommodate syllable/akshara units. (ii) "decodability" itself is a weaker constraint in shallow orthographies (almost everything is decodable early), so the *value-add* shifts from "is it decodable" to "is it sequenced and culturally authentic" — worth stating so non-English work is scoped to where it actually helps.

**(f) Leveling — conflation risk.** The plan treats "level" as a step in *one* sequence. But "leveling" in the field also means difficulty (sentence length, text volume, decoding load *within* the allowed GPC set). Two books at the same sequence-step can differ wildly in difficulty. **Recommend** a small set of additional book metadata (running-word count, sentence-length band, new-GPC-density) so a "set" is coherent in difficulty, not just in GPC coverage. This also makes the books more useful to partners who map to their own levels.

**(g) Illustrations / licensing — handled well; one practical risk.** Treating illustrations as a *separate rights surface* with per-image provenance, the AI-image "off by default, labeled" stance, and the "originals or verified PD/open" default are all correct and conservative (AI-generated image copyright is genuinely unsettled — the plan is right not to lean on it). **Practical risk:** sourcing *open-licensed, culturally-appropriate, consistent-style* illustrations at volume is the likely real bottleneck (a 6-book set needs dozens of coherent images). The plan has no illustration *supply* strategy beyond "commissioned/contributed/PD." Bloom and African Storybook solve this with shared CC-licensed art pools and "shell book" art reuse — the plan should plan to draw on those (see §3).

**(h) Accessibility — strong, with a licensing snag.** EPUB3 + semantic HTML + print PDF + large-print/low-ink + clean structure for future audio alignment is a genuinely good, often-neglected commitment. **Snag:** "dyslexia-friendly font option" — OpenDyslexic is open (SIL OFL/Bitstream-Vera-style), but many dyslexia fonts are proprietary, and the *efficacy* of dyslexia-specific fonts is contested in research. Specify the actual open font and frame it as an *option*, not an evidence-based intervention. Coordinate with the sibling `open-accessible-fonts` project (the plan already lists it).

**(i) Scope vs. `literacy-from-zero` / `open-childrens-books` — boundaries are clean.** The non-goals draw crisp lines (decodables here; non-decodable picture books in `open-childrens-books`; narration in `read-aloud-audio`; instruction in `literacy-from-zero`). This is correct and avoids duplication. **One dependency risk:** the project's *primary* value realization (Definition of Shipped = "actually used with real learners") is gated on a partner that does not yet exist, and the most natural partner (`literacy-from-zero`) is itself a flagship-in-progress. The plan is honest about this (`verifiedNeed=false`), but it means M0/M1 deliver *infrastructure and sample content* whose real-world value is unproven until M2. That's an acceptable cold-start posture — just don't let the book count grow faster than partner interest (the plan's M1 pause/decision point correctly guards this).

**Completeness verdict:** All 17 required sections present; gates are enforceable; risks own named mitigations; honesty about partner/verifiedNeed is exemplary. The two most material correctness findings are **(a) the lexicon/checker's handling of context-dependent GPCs and tokenization** (a silent "false-pass" hole the plan's own risk table flags but the data model under-specifies) and **(c) the absence of a gate-blocking story-quality rubric** distinct from decodability.

No one ships *open + scope-mapped + machine-verified + natively multilingual + illustrated* decodable sets.

## 3. Gaps we can fill

1. **A decodability *guarantee* nobody else offers.** Every competitor's "decodable" is an editorial claim. A published, open **decodability linter + scope-and-sequence-as-data + segmented lexicon** that *deterministically verifies* each book is a category-defining artifact — reusable by GDL, Bloom authors, and the free-PDF ecosystem to *certify* their own content. This is the wedge.
2. **License-clean, redistributable, *commercial-reuse-OK* decodables (CC-BY/CC0).** Most "free" decodables forbid redistribution/derivatives or are NC (CKLA). True CC-BY/CC0 decodables can be printed locally, sold in local book markets (GDL's stated goal), translated-as-derivatives, and embedded in other curricula. That freedom is scarce and high-value in low-resource settings.
3. **Scope-and-sequence *mapped as data*, supporting multiple orders.** No open project publishes machine-readable, versioned sequences that books are checked against and that partners can align to their own order. This is reusable infrastructure beyond our own books.
4. **Natively-authored, orthography-aware decodables for under-served languages.** The biggest gap by far: open decodable *sets* mapped to a *native* sequence for shallow-orthography and minority languages barely exist. Bloom enables this per-author but doesn't curate verified sets; GDL/ASB/StoryWeaver have the languages but not the decodability discipline. Doing it *right* (native sequence, native author, native reviewer, verified) is unoccupied territory.
5. **Provenance + accessibility as first-class, not afterthoughts.** Per-image/per-source license provenance, EPUB3/large-print/low-ink, and clean structure for downstream audio alignment — a discipline the free-PDF world conspicuously lacks.

---

## 4. Differentiators to win

1. **The decodability linter is the moat.** "Decodability you can verify, not just claim" is a differentiator *and* a public good. Ship it as a standalone, well-documented open tool (+ an MCP server, §7) so others certify against it — that drives adoption and makes us the de-facto standard for *what "decodable" means as data*.
2. **Open license that actually permits reuse (CC-BY/CC0), with airtight provenance.** Beats Flyleaf/Reading Universe/CKLA-NC on freedom and beats the PDF ecosystem on trustworthiness.
3. **Multilingual done the *right* way.** Native sequence + native author + native reviewer + verification is a credibility differentiator no aggregator matches.
4. **Distribution via incumbents, not against them.** Publish *into* GDL, Bloom, StoryWeaver, African Storybook (the plan already targets these). We win by being the verified, open, scope-mapped *content + tooling* layer that those platforms lack — partner, don't compete.
5. **Accessibility + integration with `literacy-from-zero`.** Being the purpose-built decodable practice tier for a structured-literacy flagship is a use-case no general decodable publisher serves.

## 5. Claude API leverage (and hard limits)

The funded lane is out of scope for v0.1 (donated lane only), so any Claude use is the human's own interactive agent during authoring — but the *capabilities* below are where an Anthropic model (default **`claude-opus-4-8`**; for the highest-stakes multilingual/long-horizon work, `claude-fable-5`) adds the most leverage. If a metered "surge authoring" lane is ever added (open question 7), these become the funded-lane workload — always under a hard per-task budget cap and the same gates.

**Where Claude helps (candidate generation, never final authority):**
1. **Constrained decodable-text candidate generation.** Given an allowed GPC set + tricky-word list for step *k*, generate *multiple* candidate short stories that try to stay in-vocabulary. This is the highest-value use: it turns the brutal constraint-satisfaction-plus-creativity problem into "generate 10, lint all, human picks the best." Pair with **structured outputs** (`output_config.format`) to return story + word list + the model's own flagged "reach" words in one schema-validated object. The model will *not* get decodability perfectly right — that's fine; the linter is the judge.
2. **Leveling / difficulty shaping.** Propose sentence-length bands, running-word counts, and within-level difficulty grading (per finding §1f); suggest which step a draft actually fits.
3. **Illustration-brief generation.** Draft per-page illustration briefs (scene, characters, mood, cultural cues, accessibility notes) for human artists or open-art search — *briefs only*, not images (AI-image policy is off-by-default).
4. **Lexicon seeding & segmentation candidates.** Propose candidate grapheme→phoneme segmentations for new words (seeded from PD CMUdict for English) for a human to verify — accelerates the lexicon without auto-accepting.
5. **Reviewer-support tooling.** Summarize a draft's flagged words, surface possible cultural/age-appropriateness concerns for human attention, draft checklist scaffolding.

**Where Claude must NOT decide (hard guardrails — these are the project's integrity):**
- **Decodability is verified by the deterministic linter, never by the LLM.** The model may *attempt* to write in-vocabulary and *flag* its own reaches, but the pass/fail verdict comes only from the lexicon-backed checker. An LLM "thinks this is decodable" is exactly the false-pass hole the plan's risk table calls out.
- **Lexicon segmentations are human-reviewed, never auto-accepted.** Claude proposes; a pedagogy/linguistic reviewer confirms. The checker blocks on `needs-segmentation`.
- **Pedagogy + cultural-appropriateness are expert/native-reviewer gated.** For any non-English work, a native-speaker literacy reviewer is mandatory; an LLM's fluency in a language is not a substitute and must not gate a book.
- **No unreviewed child-facing content ships, ever.** Safeguarding/representation sign-off (independent of the drafter) is non-negotiable; the model never auto-publishes.
- **Story quality is human-judged.** Per finding §1c — "engaging" is not a metric the model scores its own output on for the gate.
- **No secrets/keys in logs or receipts** (CLAUDE.md) — applies to any funded-lane metering if ever introduced.

## 6. Ten concrete optimizations

1. **Make the lexicon key (grapheme, phoneme) tuples and allow multiple entries per spelling** (homographs/heteronyms/context-dependent GPCs). Without this the checker can false-pass `c`=/s/ at a step that only taught `c`=/k/. *(Addresses §1a — highest priority.)*
2. **Write a tokenization spec** (capitals, contractions, hyphenates, proper nouns/names, inflections, numerals, punctuation) into the content policy and checker before M0's first book. "100% coverage" is undefined without it.
3. **Add a gate-blocking story-quality rubric** (engagement/arc/character/age-fit), human-scored, separate from decodability — so checker-passed books can still be rejected for being lifeless. *(Addresses §1c.)*
4. **Name the M0 English sequence shortlist now** (recommend **UFLI Foundations** order and/or a Letters-and-Sounds-derived order) and *verify each candidate's reuse license* before deriving — UFLI is "free to use" but lacks a clear CC derivatives grant.
5. **Add difficulty metadata to `book.yaml`** (running-word count, sentence-length band, new-GPC density) so a "set" is coherent in difficulty, not just GPC coverage. *(Addresses §1f.)*
6. **Declare the orthography scope of v1 explicitly** (alphabetic/phonemic) and design the schema to later accommodate abugidas/abjads/syllabaries/tone, so non-English M2 doesn't hit a data-model wall. *(Addresses §1e.)*
7. **Specify the actual open dyslexia font** (e.g., OpenDyslexic under its open license) and frame it as an *option*, not an evidence-based intervention; coordinate with `open-accessible-fonts`. *(Addresses §1h.)*
8. **Ship the checker as a standalone, documented, versioned tool + MCP server** (§7) so GDL/Bloom/PDF-ecosystem authors can certify their own content — turning our gate into the field's standard and a distribution flywheel.
9. **Secure an illustration supply strategy** before scaling book count: shared CC art pools (African Storybook, Bloom shell-book art, openverse/Wikimedia), a contributor-artist pipeline, and a consistent-style guide — illustration sourcing is the likely real M1 bottleneck. *(Addresses §1g.)*
10. **Pre-commit a partner-outreach gate at end of M1** (already in the plan as a pause/decision point) and *cap book production until ≥1 partner conversation is serious* — avoid authoring sets no program has committed to use (the plan's own top risk).

## 7. Parallel & perpendicular spin-offs

- **A shared `decodability-engine` (parallel, highest-leverage).** Extract the checker + sequence schema + lexicon format into a standalone open library/CLI used across the literacy portfolio. It's reusable by `literacy-from-zero` (to verify its own practice texts), by `open-childrens-books` (to *tag* which of its books happen to be decodable at which level), and by external platforms (GDL/Bloom/PDF authors) to certify content. This is the single most reusable artifact the project produces.
- **An MCP server exposing the engine** (perpendicular). Tools: `check_decodability(text, sequenceId, step)`, `list_allowed_gpcs(sequenceId, step)`, `segment_word(word) → candidate (human-review-required)`, `suggest_in_vocab_synonyms(word, step)`. This lets *any* agent (the donated-lane author's agent, or a partner's) author against the constraint interactively and verify deterministically — and makes "decodability as a service" a public good. (Build per the claude-api skill: standard tool-use surface; the linter stays deterministic server-side.)
- **`literacy-from-zero` integration (parallel, the flagship tie).** Books become its "now read a whole story" tier; the engine verifies the tier. This is the plan's primary outcome path — formalize the data contract (which sequence, which steps) early.
- **`read-aloud-audio` alignment (perpendicular).** The plan already keeps clean structured text for future narration alignment. Decodable texts are an ideal first corpus for word-level audio alignment (small controlled vocab); a shared timing/alignment format benefits both.
- **`open-pronunciation-audio` (perpendicular).** Per-GPC and per-tricky-word pronunciation audio, keyed to the sequence, supports both the lexicon (audio reference for segmentations) and learners; reuses the same sequence-as-data spine.
- **`world-folktales-open` / `public-domain-translations` (perpendicular source feed).** Verified-PD folktales become *adaptation* candidates for higher-level decodables (where vocab constraints loosen), with the licensing discipline the plan already specifies.
- **A "certify your decodable" service / badge (parallel, ecosystem play).** Offer the open community a way to run their PDFs/books through the engine and earn a verifiable "decodable at sequence X, step k" provenance stamp — drives adoption of *our* sequences-as-data as the lingua franca.

## 8. Open questions

1. **Context-dependent GPC modeling:** Will the lexicon key on (grapheme, phoneme) tuples with per-sense entries, and how will the checker disambiguate homographs in running text (POS? human tagging? block-and-review)? *(Blocks correctness of the headline gate.)*
2. **Tokenization & proper-noun policy:** Are character names required to be decodable, pre-taught as tricky, or exempt? How are contractions/inflections/numerals scored? *(Needed before the first book.)*
3. **Sequence licensing reality:** Is UFLI's order actually reusable/derivable under an open license, or only "free to use"? Same question for any candidate order — the plan must verify, not assume, before deriving.
4. **"100% decodable" external messaging vs. industry's ~80% convention:** how to claim our stricter coverage gate without overclaiming relative to Geodes/CKLA, given tricky words do real work?
5. **Illustration supply at volume:** commissioned vs. CC-art-pool reuse (ASB/Bloom) vs. contributor pipeline — what's the actual M1 plan, and the consistent-style approach?
6. **Story-quality gate:** who judges, against what rubric, and is it truly gate-blocking?
7. **First partner / `literacy-from-zero` data contract:** which sequence and steps does the flagship actually need, and when does that constraint land (it shapes M0's sequence choice)?

### ~300-word summary

**Top 3 competitors.** (1) **Global Digital Library** (Norad/UNESCO/NDLA) — genuinely open (CC-BY/CC-BY-SA), multilingual, hosts "leveled and decodable" books, and is a natural distribution channel, but only *aggregates* and offers **no decodability verification**. (2) **Bloom Library (SIL)** — the closest analogue: free OSS authoring of decodable+leveled mother-tongue readers across 500–1000+ languages, but decodability is author-asserted, not externally verified, with no canonical sequence-as-data. (3) **UFLI Foundations** — free, transparent, widely-adopted English scope-and-sequence with free decodable passages; the strongest *sequence source* for M0, though its reuse license needs verification and it's English passages, not illustrated open sets. (Flyleaf/Reading Universe/Geodes/CKLA are higher-craft but English-only and not openly reusable — CKLA is NC.)

**Single strongest differentiator.** A **published, deterministic decodability linter + scope-and-sequence-as-data + segmented lexicon** that turns "decodable" from an editorial claim into a verifiable, open guarantee — usable by the whole open ecosystem to certify their own content. Nobody else has this.

**Top 3 Claude-API ideas.** (1) Generate *multiple* decodable story candidates constrained to a step's GPC set, return via structured outputs with self-flagged "reach" words, let the linter judge. (2) Draft per-page illustration *briefs* (not images). (3) Propose candidate lexicon segmentations (CMUdict-seeded) and within-level difficulty leveling — all human-verified. Hard limit: the LLM never decides decodability, pedagogy, cultural-appropriateness, or story quality, and never publishes child-facing content unreviewed.

**Two most important plan-correctness findings.** (1) The lexicon/checker data model under-specifies **context-dependent GPCs and tokenization** (homographs like `c`=/k/ vs /s/, contractions, proper nouns) — a silent false-pass hole in the headline gate that must be closed before the first book. (2) There is **no gate-blocking story-quality rubric** distinct from decodability, so "checker-passed" books can be correct but lifeless — the perennial decodable-text failure mode.
